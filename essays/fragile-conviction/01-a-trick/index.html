      1. Conviction Without Ground

      You are convinced something happened.

      [Example of two matches, one black. Tell the story from the observers side, and from the performer side]

      A coin disappears from a hand. A card appears where it should not. The sequence is clear, immediate, and compelling. There is no hesitation in what you take to have occurred.

      Then the method is revealed.

      Nothing impossible happened. No laws were broken. The sequence you experienced was constructed. Key steps were hidden, attention was directed, and alternatives never entered consideration.

      What is striking is not that you were deceived.

      It is that the conviction felt complete.

      You did not merely consider a possibility. You relied on it, if only for a moment. The experience produced a conviction strong enough to guide judgment, even though it could not withstand inspection.

      2. Asymmetry

      The same event supports two different positions.

      The observer is convinced that something impossible occurred.
      The performer is not.

      This difference does not arise from disagreement about what happened in some abstract sense. It arises from a difference in access.

      One sees a structured sequence with hidden constraints.
      The other sees only the surface.

      Conviction, in this case, is not determined solely by the event. It depends on how the event is made available.

      Conviction can be strong, coherent, and immediate, while depending on restricted access to its own formation.

      3. A Tempting Idea

      One natural response is this:

      “If all steps were visible, the conviction would not occur.”

      This captures something important. The conviction seems tied to what was not seen.

      But as a general criterion, it fails.

      Different people would react differently even with full access.
      What counts as “all steps” is unclear.
      In most cases, full access is not available at all.

      The idea points in the right direction, but it relies on a counterfactual that cannot be fixed or tested in a stable way.

      4. A Foil: Counterfactuals in Causality

      In other domains, similar problems are handled differently.

      In causal inference, we often cannot observe what would have happened under different conditions. Instead of relying on individual counterfactuals, we compare groups and estimate an average treatment effect.

      This works because outcomes can be measured and compared across cases.

      Conviction is different.

      There is no stable outcome variable.
      There is no shared baseline.
      Convictions depend on prior structures, attention, trust, and history.

      Averaging across people does not recover a meaningful counterfactual for a single conviction.

      The problem is not only that the counterfactual is unknown. It is that conviction is not the kind of thing that can be compared in this way.

      5. From Outcomes to Structure

      If counterfactual outcomes do not provide a stable criterion, the focus has to shift.

      Not:

      What would someone believe under different conditions?

      But:

      How does this conviction form and stabilize?

      Across domains, certain patterns repeatedly support stable conviction:

      • the possibility of inspecting the steps involved
      • the ability to repeat the process
      • the detection and correction of errors
      • the availability of the process to others

      These are not guarantees of truth. They are conditions under which conviction becomes robust.

      6. Fragile Conviction

      The earlier example can now be described more precisely.

      The conviction formed there depends on:

      • restricted access to key steps
      • controlled attention
      • absence of error detection
      • asymmetry between participants

      It stabilizes locally, but cannot withstand variation in conditions.

      This suggests a distinction:

      Conviction formation is fragile when it stabilizes under conditions that do not allow meaningful inspection, challenge, or revision.

      Fragility is not about being false.
      It is about depending on conditions that prevent correction.

      7. A Simple Diagnostic

      Fragile conviction often shows recognizable features.

      A conviction is at risk when it depends on:

      1. Restricted access
        Key elements of the process are hidden or difficult to examine.

      2. Asymmetry
        Some participants could identify the issue, others cannot.

      3. Lack of corrigibility
        The conviction does not adjust proportionally when challenged.

      4. Premature closure
        Alternatives are excluded before they are adequately explored.

      These are not strict criteria. They are recurring patterns.

      They indicate that conviction may be stabilizing without the support structures that normally allow it to be tested.

      8. Magic as a Controlled Case

      Magic tricks are not only entertainment. They are controlled demonstrations.

      They isolate a specific phenomenon:

      Conviction can form quickly and strongly when correction mechanisms are selectively removed.

      Nothing special is required beyond ordinary cognitive processes.

      Perception, attention, expectation, and pattern completion are sufficient.

      What is removed is not the capacity to reason, but the conditions under which reasoning can correct itself.

      9. Not All Asymmetry Is Fragility

      The presence of asymmetry does not by itself indicate fragility.

      Many ordinary situations involve unequal access:

      • learning from a teacher
      • relying on instruments
      • trusting expertise

      In such cases, the key question is different:

      Is the asymmetry in principle reducible and open to correction?

      A scientific instrument can be calibrated.
      An expert can be questioned or replaced.
      A procedure can be inspected, at least indirectly.

      Where such pathways exist, conviction can still be robust, even if access is uneven in practice.

      Fragility arises when these pathways are absent, blocked, or ignored.

      10. Robustness

      The contrast to fragility is not certainty.

      It is robustness.

      A conviction is robust when it remains stable under reasonable variation in perspective, information, and scrutiny.

      This does not require universal agreement.
      It does not require complete transparency.

      It requires that the conviction does not depend on narrowly constrained conditions that prevent it from being tested.

      11. Two Difficult Cases

      Placebo Effects

      A patient improves after treatment. The improvement is real. The mechanism is not what was believed.

      Conviction plays a causal role. The outcome is not illusory.

      At the same time, the formation of the conviction is asymmetric. The patient does not have access to the mechanisms at work in the same way a researcher might.

      This produces an unusual combination:

      The conviction is effective.
      The formation is fragile.

      Fragility does not imply uselessness. It describes how the conviction depends on restricted conditions.

      In response, medical practice introduces mechanisms designed to counter this fragility:

      • control groups
      • blinding
      • replication

      These do not eliminate conviction. They restructure the conditions under which it forms.

      Statistical Reasoning

      Statistical methods are among the most robust tools we have.

      They rely on:

      • explicit procedures
      • repeatability
      • shared standards
      • formal constraints

      At the same time, statistical claims are easily misrepresented.

      Presentation can introduce fragility:

      • selective framing
      • omitted assumptions
      • misleading visualizations
      • cherry-picked results

      The same domain can therefore produce both robust and fragile conviction.

      Fragility is not a property of a field. It is a property of how a particular conviction is formed.

      12. Closing

      Convictions are not chosen. They form under conditions that are only partly visible and not fully controllable.

      But those conditions can be attended to.

      One cannot guarantee that a conviction is correct.
      One can ask whether it is fragile or robust.

      This shifts evaluation away from unreachable standards of truth, and toward the structure of conviction formation itself.

      The problem is not that conviction can be misleading.

      The problem is that it can stabilize in ways that prevent us from noticing when it is.

      Conviction becomes unreliable when it depends on conditions that prevent it from being examined.

      Or more simply:

      Conviction is trustworthy when it can survive its own inspection.
